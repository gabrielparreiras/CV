# Case: Cloud Cost Optimization and FinOps Governance

## Context

Cloud adoption brings scalability, speed and flexibility, but without governance it can also create uncontrolled recurring OPEX. In enterprise environments, cloud consumption needs to be managed as both an engineering and financial discipline.

## Challenge

Improve cloud cost efficiency while preserving resilience, delivery speed, scalability and business agility.

Common problems addressed by a FinOps discipline:

- Overprovisioned infrastructure.
- Idle or underused resources.
- Lack of tagging and allocation rules.
- Unclear ownership of cloud consumption.
- Disconnected budget planning and engineering decisions.
- Recurring costs hidden in product or platform usage.

## Approach

The approach connected finance, engineering, operations, architecture and product decisions.

Key practices:

- Created visibility over consumption patterns and cost drivers.
- Prioritized optimization opportunities by financial impact and operational risk.
- Improved governance around resource allocation, tagging, lifecycle and ownership.
- Connected FinOps with architecture reviews and engineering planning.
- Used operational routines to continuously identify waste, underutilization and scaling opportunities.
- Promoted cost awareness as part of engineering culture.

## Business Impact

- Savings above R$10MM/year through cloud and infrastructure optimization initiatives.
- Better connection between engineering decisions and OPEX impact.
- Improved predictability and governance over technology consumption.
- Stronger executive visibility of cost, value and operational efficiency.

## Leadership Lessons

- Cloud does not eliminate management; it demands more management.
- FinOps should enter early in architecture, product design, SRE practices and budget planning.
- The best question is not only “How much are we spending?” but “How much business value are we converting for each unit of cloud spend?”
- Cost optimization should not become reliability degradation; financial efficiency must be balanced with availability, performance and risk.
